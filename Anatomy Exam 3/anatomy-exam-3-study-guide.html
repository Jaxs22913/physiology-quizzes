Gastrointestinal System I & II

The abdominal cavity mapped region by region — how the peritoneum drapes the organs, the tube from stomach to anus, the solid organs that feed into it, and the vessels and lymphatics that supply and drain the whole tract.

IO 1 Regions & quadrants of the abdomen

The abdomen is mapped two ways. The simple clinical map uses four quadrants drawn by a vertical and a horizontal line crossing at the umbilicus. The detailed map uses nine regions, drawn by two vertical midclavicular lines and two horizontal lines (the subcostal line across the lowest ribs and the transtubercular line across the tops of the hip bones).

Memory hook Down the midline, top to bottom: Epigastric → Umbilical → Hypogastric. "Above the stomach, around the navel, below the navel."

IO 2 Parietal vs. visceral peritoneum

The peritoneum is a serous membrane lining the abdomen, like a balloon pressed in by the organs. The parietal peritoneum lines the abdominal wall; the visceral peritoneum wraps the organs. The slit of fluid-filled space between the two layers is the peritoneal cavity, divided into a large greater sac and a smaller lesser sac (omental bursa) tucked behind the stomach.

Intraperitoneal (mobile, mesentery)Retroperitoneal (fixed, behind)
Stomach, spleen, liver, most of small intestine (jejunum, ileum), transverse & sigmoid colonMost of duodenum, pancreas, ascending & descending colon, kidneys, adrenal glands, abdominal aorta, IVC
Memory hook Retroperitoneal structures — SAD PUCKER: Suprarenals, Aorta/IVC, Duodenum (2nd–4th), Pancreas, Ureters, Colon (ascending/descending), Kidneys, Esophagus, Rectum.

IO 3 Cell types, tissues & organs of the GI tract

From the esophagus to the anus, the gut wall is built from the same four concentric layers (tunics). Knowing the generic wall lets you predict what changes region to region.

The epithelium changes with function along the tract:

  • Esophagus — non-keratinized stratified squamous (abrasion protection for swallowed food).
  • Stomach & intestines — simple columnar (secretion and absorption).
  • Anal canal (distal) — back to stratified squamous at the anal verge.

Accessory organs (teeth, tongue, salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, pancreas) are not part of the tube but empty secretions into it. The salivary glands are three paired sets: parotid (25–30% of saliva, drains via Stensen's duct, wraps the facial nerve), submandibular (60–70%, the workhorse), and sublingual (3–5%).

IO 4 Stomach — regions & relations

The stomach is an intraperitoneal J-shaped bag with four regions and two curvatures.

Relations: anteriorly the diaphragm, left lobe of liver, and anterior abdominal wall; posteriorly the stomach bed — pancreas, spleen, left kidney and adrenal, splenic artery, and the transverse mesocolon. The lesser omentum runs from the lesser curvature to the liver; the greater omentum hangs from the greater curvature.

IO 5 Spleen & its relations

The spleen is an intraperitoneal lymphatic organ in the left hypochondrium, tucked against ribs 9–11. It filters blood: white pulp is lymphatic tissue (immune surveillance), red pulp filters and removes old red cells and is a blood reservoir.

  • Relations: diaphragm (superolateral), stomach (medial), left kidney (posterior), splenic flexure of colon (inferior), and the tail of the pancreas reaches its hilum.
  • Ligaments: gastrosplenic (to stomach, carries short gastric vessels) and splenorenal (to left kidney, carries the splenic vessels and pancreatic tail).
  • Blood supply: splenic artery (largest branch of the celiac trunk, takes a wavy course along the top of the pancreas); drained by the splenic vein, a major root of the portal vein.
Memory hook Spleen dimensions "1×3×5×7×9×11": ~1×3×5 inches, ~7 oz, behind ribs 9–11.

IO 6 Small intestine

About 6 m long, running from the pylorus to the ileocecal valve. Three parts:

PartLength / positionKey features
Duodenum~25 cm, C-shaped, mostly retroperitonealReceives bile + pancreatic juice at the major duodenal papilla (ampulla of Vater); ends at the duodenojejunal flexure (suspended by the ligament of Treitz).
Jejunum~2.5 m, proximal 40%, LUQ, intraperitonealThicker wall, wider lumen, tall plicae circulares, more vascular, longer vasa recta, fewer arterial arcades.
Ileum~3.5 m, distal 60%, RLQ, intraperitonealThinner wall, more arcades, shorter vasa recta, Peyer's patches (lymphoid follicles); ends at the ileocecal valve.
Key idea Absorptive surface is amplified three times over: circular folds (plicae circulares) → finger-like villi → microscopic microvilli (brush border). Plicae are permanent — unlike gastric rugae, they don't flatten when the gut fills.

IO 7 Large intestine

~1.5 m, framing the small intestine like a picture frame. Segments in order: cecum → ascending → transverse → descending → sigmoid colon → rectum → anal canal.

The rectum (last ~15 cm) stores feces. The anal canal has two sphincters: internal (smooth muscle, involuntary) and external (skeletal muscle, voluntary). Teniae coli are absent at the rectum and appendix.

IO 8 Pancreas

Retroperitoneal, lying across L1–L2 in the epigastric/left hypochondriac regions, with four parts.

  • Head — sits in the C-loop of the duodenum; the uncinate process hooks behind the superior mesenteric vessels (SMA/SMV).
  • Neck — overlies the SMV; the portal vein forms behind it.
  • Body — crosses the aorta, left kidney, and left adrenal.
  • Tail — the only intraperitoneal part; reaches the splenic hilum in the splenorenal ligament.

Ducts: the main pancreatic duct (Wirsung) joins the common bile duct at the ampulla of Vater → major duodenal papilla. The accessory duct (Santorini) opens separately at the minor papilla. Supply: head by pancreaticoduodenal arcades (from gastroduodenal artery above and SMA below); body and tail by the splenic artery.

IO 9 Lobes of the liver

The largest gland, in the RUQ under the diaphragm, covered by Glisson's capsule. Four anatomical lobes, best seen from the visceral (under) surface where an H-shape of grooves divides them.

Peritoneal attachments: falciform ligament (carries the ligamentum teres = obliterated umbilical vein), coronary and triangular ligaments (surround the bare area touching the diaphragm), and the lesser omentum to the lesser curvature. The porta hepatis is the gateway where the portal vein, hepatic artery, and bile duct enter/exit.

IO 10 Portal vein & portal drainage

The liver has a dual blood supply: the hepatic artery brings ~25–30% (oxygen), and the hepatic portal vein brings ~70–75% — nutrient-rich, deoxygenated blood drained from the entire GI tract for "first-pass" processing before it reaches the heart.

Memory hook "SMV + Splenic = Portal." The IMV is the quiet third partner that usually joins the splenic vein upstream.

IO 11 Gallbladder & biliary tree

The gallbladder stores and concentrates bile. It sits in a fossa on the visceral surface of the liver, between the right and quadrate lobes. Three parts: fundus (rounded tip, projects past the liver edge), body, and neck (narrows into the cystic duct).

IO 12 Abdominal aorta — branches to the GI tract

The abdominal aorta gives three unpaired midline branches to the gut, each supplying a segment defined by embryonic origin.

Artery (vertebral level)Supplies (embryonic gut)Main branches
Celiac trunk (T12)Foregut: distal esophagus → 2nd duodenum, plus liver, gallbladder, spleen, pancreasLeft gastric; splenic; common hepatic (→ proper hepatic + gastroduodenal)
Superior mesenteric (SMA) (L1)Midgut: 2nd duodenum → proximal 2/3 of transverse colonInferior pancreaticoduodenal, jejunal/ileal, ileocolic, right & middle colic

Memory hook Foregut = Celiac, Midgut = SMA, Hindgut = IMA. The watershed between SMA and IMA territory is near the splenic flexure.

IO 13 Inferior vena cava — tributaries

The IVC forms at ~L5 from the union of the two common iliac veins and ascends to the right of the aorta, piercing the diaphragm at T8 to reach the right atrium. Note the asymmetry from its embryology:

  • Directly into IVC: hepatic veins, right suprarenal vein, right gonadal (testicular/ovarian) vein, both renal veins, inferior phrenic veins, lumbar veins, common iliac veins.
  • The left side detours through the left renal vein: the left suprarenal and left gonadal veins drain into the left renal vein first, which then crosses to the IVC.
  • Important: the gut itself does not drain into the IVC directly — its venous blood goes to the portal vein → liver → hepatic veins → IVC.
Key idea The IVC drains the solid organs and body wall directly, but the hollow gut is routed through the portal system first. That two-step path is the whole reason portal hypertension backs up into the gut.

IO 14 Abdominal lymph drainage

Lymph from the GI organs follows the arteries backward to prevertebral nodes clustered at the roots of the three unpaired aortic branches, then up to a common collecting point.

Endocrine System

The glands that signal through the blood instead of nerves — where each sits, what it's made of at the tissue level, its neighbors and blood supply, and how several of them cooperate to keep blood calcium steady.

Instructional Objectives

  1. Organs of the endocrine system
  2. Histology of the endocrine organs
  3. Anatomical relationships & blood supply

IO 1 Organs of the endocrine system

Endocrine glands are ductless — they release hormones straight into the bloodstream. The lecture focuses on five main players, set within the wider system.

The pituitary (hypophysis) hangs from the hypothalamus by the infundibulum and sits in the hypophyseal fossa of the sella turcica (sphenoid bone). It has two lobes with very different origins:

  • Adenohypophysis (anterior lobe) — glandular tissue; releases ACTH, TSH, GH, PRL, FSH, LH, MSH. Fed by the hypophyseal portal system (portal blood carrying hypothalamic releasing hormones).
  • Neurohypophysis (posterior lobe) — nervous tissue; stores and releases ADH and oxytocin made in the hypothalamus. Receives arterial blood directly.
Key idea Anterior pituitary = portal blood (hormonal control). Posterior pituitary = arterial blood + direct neural axons (the hypothalamo-hypophyseal tract). This single distinction explains how each lobe is controlled.

IO 3 Anatomical relationships & blood supply

Thyroid gland — in the lower neck (C5–T1), two lobes joined by an isthmus over tracheal rings 2–3. It lies within the pretracheal fascia, so it moves with swallowing.

  • Relations: medial surface against larynx/trachea and pharynx/esophagus, with the external laryngeal and recurrent laryngeal nerves nearby; lateral surface under the infrahyoid ("strap") muscles and sternocleidomastoid; posteriorly the carotid sheath.
  • Arteries: superior thyroid (from external carotid) and inferior thyroid (from the thyrocervical trunk of the subclavian); occasionally a thyroidea ima.
  • Veins: superior + middle thyroid → internal jugular; inferior thyroid → brachiocephalic.

Parathyroid glands — usually four, on the posterior surface of the thyroid (superior pair more constant in position). Same blood supply as the thyroid (mainly inferior thyroid artery).

Pancreas — retroperitoneal; head in the duodenal C-loop, tail to the splenic hilum; supplied by pancreaticoduodenal arcades (head) and splenic artery (body/tail). (Full GI relations in GI IO 8.)

Adrenal (suprarenal) glands — cap the superior pole of each kidney inside the renal fascia. Right is pyramidal (behind liver/IVC); left is semilunar (behind stomach/pancreas). Rich triple arterial supply: superior (from inferior phrenic), middle (from aorta), inferior (from renal artery). Single vein: right → IVC directly; left → left renal vein.

Memory hook Right adrenal vein is short and drains straight into the IVC; left takes the scenic route via the left renal vein — the same left-side detour you saw with the gonadal and renal veins.

IO 4 Calcium metabolism

Three hormones from three of these glands cooperate to hold blood calcium in a narrow range — a classic push-pull.

Memory hook "PTH Pulls calcium uP; Calcitonin Calms it down (tones it down)." Parathyroid raises, thyroid C-cells lower.

Male Reproductive System

From embryonic descent of the testes to the pelvic floor that supports everything above it — the organs, their nerves and vessels, and the full pathway a sperm cell travels from formation to ejaculation.

IO 1 Embryologic development

The testes form high in the abdominal cavity near the kidneys, then descend to the scrotum. A fibrous cord, the gubernaculum testis, anchors the testis to the future scrotum; as the body grows, the gubernaculum doesn't lengthen, so it effectively "pulls" the testis downward through the inguinal canal to reach the scrotum by birth.

IO 2 Pelvic floor muscles

The pelvic diaphragm is the muscular hammock that seals the bottom of the pelvis and supports the organs. It is formed by two muscle groups:

  • Levator ani — the main sheet, itself made of three parts: puborectalis, pubococcygeus, and iliococcygeus.
  • Coccygeus (ischiococcygeus) — the smaller posterior part.

Below the pelvic diaphragm, the urogenital diaphragm and perineal muscles (including the bulbospongiosus and ischiocavernosus) support the base of the penis and assist ejaculation. The pelvic floor supports pelvic organs, seals the pelvic outlet, and lifts to aid defecation.

Memory hook Levator ani parts — "Pubo-Pubo-Ilio": Puborectalis, Pubococcygeus, Iliococcygeus. Add coccygeus and you have the whole pelvic diaphragm.

IO 3 Blood supply to the pelvis & reproductive organs

The pelvis is supplied mainly by the internal iliac artery, with important contributions to the scrotum and testes from other sources.

StructureArterial supply
TestisTesticular artery — arises directly from the abdominal aorta (below the renal arteries), travels in the spermatic cord
ScrotumInternal pudendal (from internal iliac) + external pudendal (from femoral) + cremasteric/inferior epigastric (from external iliac)
Ductus deferensDeferential artery (from internal iliac)
Prostate, seminal glands, bladderBranches of the internal iliac (inferior vesical, middle rectal)
PenisInternal pudendal → deep, dorsal & bulbourethral arteries

Venous drainage of the testis is distinctive: veins form the pampiniform plexus in the cord (a heat-exchanger cooling arterial blood), which condenses into the testicular vein. The right testicular vein → IVC; the left testicular vein → left renal vein — the same left-side asymmetry seen elsewhere.

Key idea The testicular artery comes from the high aorta (because the testis started life up by the kidney), even though the testis now sits far below in the scrotum. Its blood supply is a fossil of where it developed.

IO 4 Innervation of the pelvic floor & reproductive organs

  • Pelvic floor (levator ani, external sphincters, perineum): the pudendal nerve (S2–S4) — the main somatic (voluntary) nerve of the perineum.
  • Erection: parasympathetic fibers (pelvic splanchnic nerves, S2–S4) → arterial dilation, engorgement. "Point" = Parasympathetic.
  • Emission & ejaculation: sympathetic fibers (hypogastric plexus) → smooth-muscle peristalsis of ducts and glands. "Shoot" = Sympathetic.
  • Scrotal skin sensation: ilioinguinal and genitofemoral nerves (anterior), pudendal branches (posterior).
Memory hook "Point and Shoot": Parasympathetic = erection (Point), Sympathetic = ejaculation (Shoot). The pudendal nerve handles the voluntary squeeze at the end.

IO 5 Organs & their relationships

The system is a chain of gonad → duct system → accessory glands → penis.

Testis structure: a fibrous tunica albuginea sends septa inward, dividing it into lobules of coiled seminiferous tubules. Sperm form here; between the tubules, interstitial (Leydig) cells make testosterone, and nurse (Sertoli) cells inside the tubules support sperm and form the blood-testis barrier. Tubules drain: seminiferous → straight tubules → rete testis → efferent ductules → epididymis.

Accessory glands and their contributions to semen: seminal glands (~60%, fructose + prostaglandins), prostate (~20–30%, weakly acidic, anticoagulant enzymes), bulbo-urethral (~5%, alkaline mucus that neutralizes urethral acid and lubricates).

IO 6 Pathway of semen — origin to ejaculation

Memory hook "SEVEN UP": Seminiferous tubules → Epididymis → Vas (ductus) deferens → Ejaculatory duct → (Nothing) → Urethra → Penis.

IO 7 Parts of the urethra

The male urethra (~18–20 cm) is the shared final path for both urine and semen. It has three parts from bladder to tip:

PartCourseNote
1. Prostatic urethraThrough the prostateReceives the ejaculatory ducts and prostatic ductules — where semen enters
2. Membranous urethraPierces the urogenital diaphragmShortest, least distensible; surrounded by the external urethral sphincter (voluntary); bulbo-urethral glands open just beyond it
3. Spongy (penile) urethraRuns the length of the penis in the corpus spongiosumLongest; ends at the external urethral orifice

The penis has three erectile columns: two dorsal corpora cavernosa (their proximal crura anchor to the ischial rami) and one ventral corpus spongiosum (surrounds the spongy urethra; its bulb anchors, its tip expands into the glans).

IO 1 Embryologic development

The internal female tract develops from the paired Müllerian (paramesonephric) ducts. They form from coelomic epithelium beside the mesonephros around week 6 and grow downward. In females — with no anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH) to make them regress — they persist and differentiate.

Memory hook The suspensory ligament is the ovary's lifeline — it's the one carrying the ovarian artery and vein. The ovarian ligament (to the uterus) carries nothing important.

IO 4 Pelvic floor muscles

Same pelvic diaphragm as in the male: the levator ani (puborectalis, pubococcygeus, iliococcygeus) plus the coccygeus. In the female it is pierced by the urethra, vagina, and anal canal. It supports the pelvic organs, resists rises in abdominal pressure, and helps maintain continence. Additional muscles around the vagina include the pubovaginalis (part of levator ani), the external urethral sphincter, the urethrovaginal sphincter, and the bulbospongiosus.

Key idea Because the female pelvic floor has an extra opening (the vagina) that must stretch during childbirth, it's more vulnerable to weakening and prolapse than the male floor — a direct anatomical consequence of that third hiatus.

IO 6 Innervation

  • Pelvic floor & perineum: pudendal nerve (S2–S4) — somatic, voluntary; the nerve stretched or blocked in childbirth.
  • Parasympathetic (S2–S4, pelvic splanchnic nerves): to uterus, tubes, vagina, and clitoral erectile tissue.
  • Sympathetic: from the uterovaginal and ovarian plexuses.
  • Ovaries: autonomic via the ovarian plexus; visceral afferent (pain) fibers from the ovary travel back with sympathetics to T10–T11 — which is why ovarian pain can refer to the periumbilical region.

IO 7 Organs & their relationships

Uterus regions: fundus (top, above the tube entrances), body, isthmus (narrowing), and cervix (into the vagina, via internal os → cervical canal → external os). Uterine wall layers: perimetrium (serosa), myometrium (thick smooth muscle), endometrium (hormone-responsive lining that cycles). Tube parts (lateral → medial): infundibulum (with fimbriae) → ampulla (site of fertilization) → isthmus → intramural/uterine part.

IO 9 Peritoneal coverings of the internal genitalia

  • Uterus: covered by peritoneum on its fundus and body (the perimetrium is this serosa), continuous with the broad ligament; the anterior surface reflects onto the bladder (vesicouterine pouch), the posterior onto the rectum (rectouterine pouch).
  • Uterine tubes: enclosed within the free upper edge of the broad ligament (the mesosalpinx) — well covered by peritoneum.
  • Ovary: notably not covered by peritoneum — it is attached to the back of the broad ligament by the mesovarium but its surface is bare germinal epithelium, so a released oocyte enters the peritoneal cavity briefly before the fimbriae catch it.
  • Cervix & vagina: largely sub-peritoneal (below the peritoneal reflection); only the posterior vaginal fornix touches the peritoneum of the rectouterine pouch.
Memory hook The ovary is the one organ that pokes through the peritoneum rather than being wrapped by it — which is exactly why ovulation releases the egg into the peritoneal cavity for the fimbriae to sweep up.

Nephrology / Urinary System

The organs that make and carry urine — the kidney's layered wrapping and internal architecture, the exact path blood takes to be filtered, the nephron segment by segment, and the ureters, bladder, and urethra that move urine out.

IO 1 Organs of the urinary system

Four organs, two jobs. The kidneys are the primary organs — they filter waste from blood and turn filtrate into urine. The urinary tract — ureters, bladder, urethra — transports urine out. The act of urination is micturition.

IO 2 Anatomy of the kidneys

Retroperitoneal organs, ~11 cm long. The right kidney sits lower (pushed down by the liver): roughly T12–L3 on the right, T10/11–L2 on the left. Vessels and the renal pelvis enter/exit at the medial hilum.

Memory hook Kidney wrappings deep→superficial: "capsule, fat, fascia, fat, peritoneum" — a fat sandwich with fascia in the middle, held between the fibrous capsule and the peritoneum.

IO 3 Blood flow to the kidney & nephrons

The kidney receives a huge fraction of cardiac output. Trace the arterial path all the way to the filter, then the venous path back out.

Memory hook Arterial order: "Some Idiots Are Included Always" — Segmental, Interlobar, Arcuate, Interlobular, Afferent → glomerulus. Two capillary beds in series (glomerular, then peritubular) is the kidney's signature.

IO 4 Sections & parts of the nephron

The nephron is the kidney's functional unit: a renal corpuscle (filter) plus a renal tubule (processor).

Nephron typeShareFeatures
Cortical~85%Near cortex surface; short loops; peritubular capillaries; make "standard" urine
Juxtamedullary~15%Deep, near medulla; long loops + vasa recta; concentrate urine (recover water, Na⁺, Cl⁻)

Glomerular filtration membrane (three layers blood must cross): fenestrated endothelium → glomerular basement membrane (negatively charged, blocks albumin) → podocyte filtration slits. This lets water, ions, glucose, amino acids, and urea through while holding back cells and large/negatively-charged proteins.

Memory hook Filtrate order: "Please Let Dad Come" — PCT, Loop of Henle, DCT, Collecting duct.

IO 5 Anatomical relationships of the urinary components

Kidneys — retroperitoneal, with adrenal glands capping their superior poles. Renal vein pattern: right renal vein short → IVC; left renal vein longer, crosses the aorta and receives the left gonadal and left suprarenal veins (the recurring left-side detour).

Ureters — ~25 cm, retroperitoneal, running from renal pelvis to the posterolateral bladder base. They cross anterior to the common iliac vessels at the pelvic brim, and run beneath the gonadal vessels ("water under the bridge"). Three natural constrictions where stones lodge: (1) ureteropelvic junction, (2) crossing of the iliac vessels/pelvic brim, (3) ureterovesical junction (entry into the bladder).

Bladder — sits just behind the pubic symphysis; subperitoneal except its superior surface, which is covered by peritoneum. The muscular wall is the detrusor; the trigone (between the two ureteric orifices and the internal urethral orifice) is a smooth triangle. The median umbilical ligament is the remnant of the urachus. Relations: in females the uterus lies posterosuperior and the vagina posteroinferior; in males the rectum is posterior and the prostate sits directly below.

Urethra — female ~3–4 cm (opens in the vestibule, anterior to the vaginal opening, posterior to the clitoris); male ~18–20 cm with three parts (prostatic, membranous, spongy — see Male IO 7).

Key idea The short female urethra and its proximity to the vestibule is the anatomical reason urinary tract infections are more common in women — bacteria have a much shorter path to the bladder.
