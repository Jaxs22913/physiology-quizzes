Appendicular Skeleton

TopicOne-glance facts
Pectoral girdleOnly 2 bones: clavicle (S-shaped; medial/sternal end → manubrium, lateral/acromial end → scapula's acromion; conoid tubercle near acromial end, costal tuberosity near sternal end) + scapula. Trades stability for mobility.
Scapula landmarksScapular spine divides supraspinous fossa (above) from infraspinous fossa (below) and continues laterally into the acromion (AC joint). Coracoid process hooks forward anteriorly; glenoid cavity (lateral) articulates with the humeral head.
HumerusHead → glenoid cavity; greater/lesser tubercles flank head (rotator-cuff attachment); deltoid tuberosity on shaft. Distally: capitulum (articulates with radius) + trochlea (articulates with ulna); coronoid & olecranon fossae accommodate the ulna in flexion/extension.
Radius & ulnaUlna is medial, forms most of elbow via trochlear notch (olecranon posterior + coronoid process anterior). Radius is lateral; radial head rotates against capitulum/radial notch; radial tuberosity = biceps brachii insertion; distal radius carries the ulnar notch. Interosseous membrane binds the two shafts.
Hand bones8 carpals (proximal row: scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, pisiform; distal row: trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate) → 5 metacarpals (1=thumb through 5=pinky) → phalanges: 3 per finger (digits 2–5), only 2 for the thumb (no middle phalanx).
Pelvic girdle2 coxal bones, each fusing ilium (superior wing) + ischium (posteroinferior, sit bone) + pubis (anteroinferior, pubic symphysis) at the acetabulum (receives femoral head). Landmarks: AIIS, lesser sciatic notch, obturator foramen, arcuate line, auricular surface (sacroiliac joint). Female pelvis is wider/shallower with larger outlet (childbirth adaptation).
Femur & patellaFemur = longest/strongest bone; head → acetabulum via the femoral neck; greater/lesser trochanters (muscle attachment) joined anteriorly by the intertrochanteric line; distal medial/lateral condyles form the knee joint with the tibia. Patella = sesamoid bone within the quadriceps tendon, apex points inferiorly toward the tibial tuberosity.
Tibia, fibula & footTibia is medial, weight-bearing, with tibial tuberosity (patellar ligament) and medial malleolus. Fibula is lateral, non-weight-bearing, forms the lateral malleolus; interosseous membrane connects the two shafts. Foot mirrors the hand: 7 tarsals (including calcaneus, the heel bone), 5 metatarsals, phalanges 2 (great toe) / 3 (other toes).

Axial Skeleton

TopicOne-glance facts
Bone tissue typesCompact bone = dense outer shell, bears most mechanical load; spongy/trabecular bone = interior lattice, concentrated near long-bone ends. Medullary cavity = central marrow space. Periosteum wraps the external surface (blood supply, tendon/ligament attachment). Osteon = compact bone's unit: concentric lamellae around a central canal, linked by perforating canals; interstitial lamellae fill gaps between osteons.
Bone classes & growth4 shapes: long (humerus), short (wrist/ankle only), flat (cranium), sesamoid (patella). Growth: primary ossification center in diaphysis (fetal) → secondary centers in epiphyses (postnatal) → epiphyseal plate (hyaline cartilage; zones: resting, proliferative, hypertrophic, calcification) drives longitudinal growth until it ossifies and fuses the epiphysis to the diaphysis.
Skull overview29 named bones: 14 facial + 8 cranial + 7 associated (hyoid, ossicles) → 80 axial + 126 appendicular = 206 total adult bones. Sutures: sagittal (between parietals), coronal (frontal–parietals), lambdoid (occipital–parietals). 3 cranial fossae: anterior (frontal lobes), middle (sella turcica/sphenoid), posterior (cerebellum, foramen magnum).
Key cranial bonesOccipital: foramen magnum + occipital condyles (articulate with atlas/C1). Frontal: supra-orbital foramen. Temporal: houses middle/inner ear, stylomastoid foramen (CN VII exit). Sphenoid: sella turcica (houses pituitary), foramen ovale/spinosum (CN V branches). Ethmoid: crista galli (falx cerebri anchor), cribriform plate (CN I fibers).
Cranial foraminaForamen magnum → spinal cord, vertebral arteries, CN XI. Optic canal → CN II. Superior orbital fissure → CN III, IV, V1, VI. Foramen rotundum → V2. Foramen ovale → V3. Foramen spinosum → middle meningeal artery. Stylomastoid foramen → CN VII. Jugular foramen (temporal + occipital) → CN IX, X, XI + internal jugular vein.
Facial bones & mandibleMaxillae = upper jaw + maxillary sinuses (1 of 4 paranasal sinuses, with frontal/sphenoidal/ethmoid). Vomer + ethmoid's perpendicular plate = bony nasal septum. Mandible = only mobile skull bone; condylar process → TMJ; coronoid process = muscle attachment; mental foramen = nerve exit. Hyoid is suspended by ligaments only (no bony articulation), anchors tongue/larynx muscles. Fontanelles (e.g. anterior) = unossified gaps allowing birth compression and brain growth.
Vertebral column overview26 bones: 7 cervical + 12 thoracic + 5 lumbar + sacrum (5 fused) + coccyx (3–5 fused). 4 alternating curves: thoracic & sacral = primary (prenatal); cervical & lumbar = secondary (postnatal). Typical vertebra: body + vertebral arch (pedicles/laminae) enclosing the vertebral foramen; spinous + transverse processes; superior/inferior articular processes.
Regional vertebrae & thoraxCervical (C1–C7): transverse foramen + bifid spinous process (except C7). C1/atlas: no body, articulates with occipital condyles ('yes'). C2/axis: dens pivots inside atlas ('no'). C7: long palpable spinous process. Thoracic (T1–T12): costal facets for ribs. Lumbar (L1–L5): largest, thickest bodies. Ribs: true 1–7 (direct to sternum), false 8–10 (indirect, vertebrochondral) & 11–12 (floating). Sternum: manubrium + body + xiphoid process.

Appendicular Musculature

TopicOne-glance facts
Axial vs. appendicular muscleAxial muscles position the head/vertebral column and move the rib cage; appendicular muscles stabilize/move the limbs and girdles, absorb shock during locomotion, and reinforce joints. Action line crossing a joint: anterior = flexor, posterior = extensor, medial = adductor/medial rotator, lateral = abductor/lateral rotator.
Scapula positionersTrapezius, rhomboid major/minor, levator scapulae, serratus anterior, pectoralis minor, subclavius. Trapezius has the broadest range (elevate/depress/rotate scapula-clavicle, extend neck). Serratus anterior protracts the shoulder and rotates the scapula (glenoid faces up); innervated by the long thoracic nerve — a lesion causes a 'winged scapula.'
Arm moversPectoralis major: flexion, adduction, medial rotation. Deltoid: abduction. Coracobrachialis: adduction. Latissimus dorsi: extension, adduction, medial rotation.
Rotator cuffSupraspinatus: abduction. Infraspinatus: lateral rotation. Teres minor: adduction + lateral rotation. Subscapularis: medial rotation. All four stabilize the humeral head within the glenoid cavity.
Elbow & forearm moversElbow flexors — biceps brachii, brachialis, brachioradialis — all originate on the humerus. Elbow extensor triceps brachii (all 3 heads insert on the olecranon). Opposing pronator/supinator pair: pronator teres/pronator quadratus vs. supinator.
Wrist & hand musclesWrist flexors: flexor carpi radialis, flexor carpi ulnaris, palmaris longus. Extensors: extensor carpi radialis longus/brevis, extensor carpi ulnaris (radial = abduct, ulnar = adduct). Extrinsic hand muscles (forearm origin, strength/crude control) vs. intrinsic (within hand, fine control: thenar/hypothenar, lumbricals, interossei).
Thigh movers (4 groups)Gluteal group: gluteus maximus (hip extension), gluteus medius/minimus + tensor fasciae latae (abduction). Lateral rotator group: piriformis, obturators, gemelli, quadratus femoris (lateral rotation, abduction). Adductor group: adductor brevis/longus/magnus, pectineus, gracilis. Iliopsoas group: iliacus + psoas major, the primary hip flexors.
Knee moversQuadriceps femoris (vastus lateralis/medialis/intermedius + rectus femoris, which also flexes the hip): extends the leg. Hamstrings (biceps femoris, semimembranosus, semitendinosus): flex the leg, extend the hip. Sartorius flexes hip + knee; with gracilis and semitendinosus forms the pes anserine at the medial tibia.
Leg & foot moversTibialis anterior: dorsiflexion. Gastrocnemius/soleus (converge on calcaneal/Achilles tendon): plantarflexion. Tibialis posterior: plantarflexion + inversion. Fibularis longus/brevis: eversion. Intrinsic foot muscles mirror the hand's fine-control layout.

Axial Musculature

TopicOne-glance facts
Muscle coverings & NMJEpimysium (whole muscle) → perimysium (fascicles) → endomysium (individual fiber/sarcolemma). At the neuromuscular junction: motor neuron releases acetylcholine (ACh) across the synaptic cleft → binds nicotinic receptors on the motor end plate → triggers contraction → terminated by acetylcholinesterase. Schwann cells cap/support the junction.
Facial expression musclesAll innervated by CN VII (facial nerve). Mouth: orbicularis oris, zygomaticus major/minor, buccinator, risorius, mentalis, depressor labii inferioris/anguli oris, levator labii superioris. Eyes: orbicularis oculi, corrugator supercilii, levator palpebrae superioris (exception — innervated by CN III; dysfunction → ptosis). Nose: procerus, nasalis. Scalp: occipitofrontalis, temporoparietalis. Neck: platysma.
Muscles of masticationMasseter, temporalis, medial pterygoid close/elevate the jaw; lateral pterygoid is the lone jaw-opener. All four innervated by the mandibular branch of CN V. Temporalis inserts on the coronoid process; masseter inserts on the mandibular angle.
Extra-ocular & tongue/neck'LR6 SO4 3': lateral rectus (abduction) = CN VI, superior oblique (rotates eye down and out) = CN IV, remaining 4 (medial/superior/inferior rectus, inferior oblique) = CN III. Tongue muscles = CN XII except palatoglossus (CN X). Sternocleidomastoid (CN XI): bilateral = neck flexion, unilateral = rotates head to opposite side.
Swallowing musculatureInfrahyoid/suprahyoid muscles (omohyoid, sternohyoid, sternothyroid, thyrohyoid, digastric, mylohyoid) position the hyoid and larynx. Pharyngeal constrictors = CN X; laryngeal elevators = CN IX & X; palatal muscles = CN V & X.
Back musclesExtrinsic (appendicular, attach limb to trunk): superficial layer (trapezius, latissimus dorsi, levator scapulae, rhomboids) + intermediate layer (serratus posterior superior/inferior, for ventilation). Intrinsic (true deep back, act as a functional group): intermediate erector spinae — spinalis, longissimus, iliocostalis (medial→lateral) — extends the column; deep layer (multifidus, rotatores, interspinales, intertransversarii) fine-tunes extension/rotation/lateral flexion. Anteriorly: longus capitis/colli (neck flex/rotate), quadratus lumborum (lateral flexion).
Ventilation & abdominal wallExternal intercostals elevate ribs (inhalation) vs. internal intercostals depress them (exhalation); scalenes elevate, transversus thoracis depresses. Diaphragm (phrenic nerves, C3–C5): contracts/flattens/descends → inhalation, relaxes/rises → exhalation. Abdominal wall: external oblique, internal oblique, transversus abdominis compress the abdomen (obliques also depress ribs, laterally flex trunk); rectus abdominis (linea alba, tendinous inscriptions) flexes the column and depresses the ribs.
Pelvic floorInnervation S2–S4; individual muscles indistinguishable clinically, so dysfunction is worked up by imaging. Urogenital triangle (anterior): superficial transverse perineal, ischiocavernosus, bulbospongiosus, deep transverse perineal, external urethral sphincter. Anal triangle (posterior): levator ani (iliococcygeus + pubococcygeus), coccygeus, obturator internus, external anal sphincter. Levator ani + coccygeus form the pelvic diaphragm.
