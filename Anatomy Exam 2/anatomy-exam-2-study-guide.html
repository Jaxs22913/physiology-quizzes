1 · The Integumentary System

Instructional Objectives

  1. Describe the structure of the epidermis, its layers, and the basis of skin color.
  2. Describe the structure of the dermis and the subcutaneous/fascial layers deep to the skin.
  3. Describe the blood/nerve supply and accessory structures of the skin, and apply this to clinical correlates including burns, stretch marks, and tension lines.

1.1 Epidermis, Dermis & Fascia IO 1–2

The skin is the body's largest organ, providing protection (mechanical, microbial, and UV), thermoregulation, sensation, vitamin D synthesis, and a limited role in excretion (via sweat). Its outer layer, the epidermis, is avascular stratified squamous keratinized epithelium, organized (deep to superficial) into the stratum basale (germinativum) (a single layer of mitotically active stem cells that continually regenerate the epidermis, interspersed with pigment-producing melanocytes), the stratum spinosum (several layers of keratinocytes linked by desmosomes, interspersed with immune Langerhans cells), the stratum granulosum (keratinocytes accumulate keratohyalin granules as they begin to die), the stratum lucidum (a thin, translucent layer found only in thick/glabrous skin — palms and soles), and the outermost stratum corneum (many layers of dead, fully keratinized cells that continually flake off). The undulating boundary between the epidermis and dermis produces epidermal ridges (fingerprints) that increase surface area and grip and correspond to the underlying dermal papillae.

Skin color reflects several pigments together: melanin (produced by melanocytes in the stratum basale and transferred to surrounding keratinocytes, it ranges in shade and protects underlying tissue from UV damage), carotene (a yellow-orange, diet-derived pigment that accumulates in the stratum corneum and subcutaneous fat), and the hemoglobin in dermal blood vessels contributing a pink/red hue when visible through lightly pigmented skin.

The dermis lies beneath the epidermis in two layers: a superficial papillary layer (loose areolar connective tissue forming the dermal papillae, rich in capillary loops and touch receptors like Meissner corpuscles) and a deeper, thicker reticular layer (dense irregular connective tissue with interwoven collagen and elastic fibers, providing the skin's structural strength and elasticity). Deep to the dermis — and not technically part of the skin itself — the subcutaneous tissue (hypodermis, superficial fascia) is a layer of areolar and adipose connective tissue that binds the skin loosely to underlying structures, providing insulation, energy storage, and cushioning (with Pacinian corpuscles at its deeper margin). Bundles of collagen fibers called the retinacula cutis (skin ligaments) cross the subcutaneous layer to anchor the dermis down to the deeper deep fascia — a dense, well-organized sheet of connective tissue that wraps individual muscles and muscle groups, forming the fascial planes that separate the body's fascial compartments from one another and are surgically important dissection landmarks.

1.2 Blood/Nerve Supply, Accessory Structures & Clinical Correlates IO 3

The skin's blood supply runs in two horizontal plexuses — a deeper cutaneous plexus at the dermal-hypodermal junction and a more superficial subpapillary plexus — whose vasodilation or vasoconstriction is a primary mechanism of thermoregulation, shunting blood toward or away from the skin surface to release or conserve heat. Its nerve supply includes free nerve endings (pain, temperature) and the encapsulated mechanoreceptors introduced in Section 5 (Meissner, Pacinian, Merkel, Ruffini), giving the skin its role as the body's largest sensory organ.

The skin's accessory structures include: hair, produced by a follicle wrapped around a hair bulb/papilla, with a smooth muscle arrector pili attached to each follicle that produces "goosebumps" when it contracts; sebaceous glands, holocrine glands usually opening into a hair follicle that secrete oily sebum to lubricate hair and skin; sweat (exocrine) glands, split into widespread eccrine glands (open directly onto the skin surface, the primary thermoregulatory sweat glands) and apocrine glands (confined to the axillae and groin, becoming active at puberty and contributing to body odor); and nails, a hardened keratin nail body/plate sitting on the nail bed, growing from the actively dividing nail matrix beneath the crescent-shaped lunula, with a cuticle (eponychium) sealing its proximal edge.

These layers underlie several clinical correlates: burn severity is classified by depth — first-degree burns involve the epidermis only (e.g., sunburn, no blistering), second-degree burns extend into the dermis (painful, blistering), and third-degree burns destroy the full thickness of the skin (often painless at the burn center due to nerve destruction, frequently requiring grafting). Stretch marks (striae) result from tearing of dermal collagen and elastic fibers when the skin is stretched faster than the reticular layer can remodel (rapid growth, pregnancy, weight change). Tension lines (Langer's lines) reflect the predominant orientation of collagen fibers within the reticular dermis; surgical incisions made parallel to these lines heal with less scarring than incisions that cut across them.

2 · Eye and Ear Anatomy

Instructional Objectives

  1. Identify the accessory structures, extrinsic muscles, and three tunics of the eye, and describe its internal cavities and chambers.
  2. Describe the histology of the retina and trace the visual pathway from retina to visual cortex.
  3. Describe the external, middle, and inner ear (including the cochlea and vestibular complex), and trace the auditory and vestibular pathways.

2.1 The Eye IO 1–2

The eye's accessory structures protect and lubricate it: the eyebrows and eyelids (palpebrae) shield the anterior eye, the conjunctiva is a thin mucous membrane lining the eyelids and covering the anterior sclera, and the lacrimal apparatus (lacrimal gland, superolateral to the eye, plus the lacrimal canaliculi, lacrimal sac, and nasolacrimal duct) produces and drains tears into the nasal cavity. The eye is moved by six extrinsic (extra-ocular) muscles — superior rectus, inferior rectus, medial rectus, lateral rectus, superior oblique, and inferior oblique — whose innervation follows the "LR6 SO4 3" rule (lateral rectus by CN VI, superior oblique by CN IV, the rest by CN III), covered in Section 6 of Exam 1's Peripheral & Cranial Nerves guide.

The eyeball wall is built from three concentric tunics. The outer fibrous tunic provides shape and protection: the white, opaque sclera covers most of the posterior eye, continuous anteriorly with the transparent, avascular cornea, which performs most of the eye's light refraction. The middle vascular tunic (uvea) is heavily pigmented and vascularized: the choroid nourishes the outer retina and absorbs stray light, the ciliary body produces aqueous humor and adjusts lens shape for accommodation via the ciliary muscle and zonule fibers, and the iris controls pupil diameter. The inner neural tunic is the retina, containing an outer pigmented layer and an inner neural layer with the photoreceptors.

Internally, the eye is divided by the lens into two cavities: the anterior cavity, filled with watery aqueous humor and further subdivided by the iris into an anterior chamber (between cornea and iris) and posterior chamber (between iris and lens), and the much larger posterior (vitreous) cavity behind the lens, filled with the gel-like vitreous humor that maintains the eyeball's shape.

The retina's photoreceptor layer contains rods (highly sensitive, dim-light/peripheral vision, no color discrimination) and cones (three subtypes tuned to different wavelengths, giving color vision and fine visual acuity, concentrated at the fovea centralis — a cone-only pit that provides the sharpest vision). By contrast, the optic disc, where retinal ganglion cell axons converge to exit as the optic nerve and retinal vessels enter/exit, contains no photoreceptors at all — the anatomic basis of each eye's natural blind spot.

The visual pathway begins as photoreceptor signals pass through retinal layers to ganglion cells, whose axons form the optic nerve (CN II). The two optic nerves meet at the optic chiasm, where fibers from each retina's nasal (medial) half cross to the opposite side while temporal (lateral) fibers stay uncrossed — the anatomic basis for how each half of the visual field is processed by the opposite cerebral hemisphere. Beyond the chiasm, the optic tract carries fibers to the lateral geniculate nucleus of the thalamus, which relays via the optic radiations to the primary visual cortex in the occipital lobe.

2.2 The Ear IO 3

The ear has three regions. The external ear — the auricle (pinna) and external acoustic meatus — collects and funnels sound to the tympanic membrane (eardrum), the boundary with the middle ear. The air-filled middle ear (tympanic cavity) houses the three smallest bones in the body, the auditory ossicles — malleus (attached to the tympanic membrane), incus, and stapes (whose footplate sits in the oval window) — which mechanically transmit and amplify vibrations from the eardrum to the inner ear. The auditory (Eustachian) tube connects the middle ear to the nasopharynx, equalizing air pressure across the tympanic membrane; the chorda tympani branch of the facial nerve (CN VII) also passes through the middle ear cavity.

The inner ear connects to the middle ear through two membrane-covered "windows": the stapes footplate transmits vibration into the inner ear at the oval window, while the round window bulges outward to relieve the resulting pressure wave, allowing perilymph to move. Structurally, a rigid bony labyrinth (filled with perilymph) houses the membranous labyrinth (filled with endolymph) suspended within it — a nested-tube arrangement shared by both the hearing and balance components of the inner ear.

The cochlea, the spiral hearing organ, is divided into three fluid-filled channels: the scala vestibuli and scala tympani (both perilymph, continuous with each other at the cochlear apex) sandwich the scala media (cochlear duct) (endolymph), which houses the organ of Corti — rows of mechanosensory hair cells resting on the basilar membrane, whose stereocilia are deflected by sound-induced vibration to generate a nerve signal. The vestibular complex senses head position and movement: the utricle and saccule (within the vestibule) detect linear acceleration and head tilt via gravity-sensitive otolith-covered hair cells, while the three fluid-filled semicircular canals (containing the membranous semicircular ducts) — oriented in three perpendicular planes — detect angular (rotational) acceleration via hair cells clustered in an ampulla at the base of each duct.

The auditory pathway runs: sound → tympanic membrane → ossicular chain → oval window → perilymph pressure wave → basilar membrane vibration → organ of Corti hair cell depolarization → the cochlear division of CN VIII (vestibulocochlear) → brainstem auditory nuclei → thalamus → auditory cortex (temporal lobe). The parallel vestibular pathway carries balance information from the utricle, saccule, and semicircular ducts via the vestibular division of CN VIII to the brainstem vestibular nuclei and cerebellum, which coordinate posture, eye movements (via the vestibulo-ocular reflex), and the subjective sense of balance.

3 · Anatomy of the Heart and Great Vessels

Instructional Objectives

  1. Describe the pericardium, heart wall, four chambers, and valves of the heart.
  2. Describe the cardiac conduction system, correlate it with the phases of the cardiac cycle, and describe its autonomic regulation.
  3. Identify the coronary vessels and trace the aorta from the heart through its major thoracic, abdominal, and pelvic/lower-limb branches.

3.1 Pericardium, Heart Wall, Chambers & Valves IO 1

The heart sits in the mediastinum, oriented with its apex pointing left, anterior, and inferior. It is enclosed by the pericardium: a tough outer fibrous pericardium anchoring the heart within the mediastinum, and a serous pericardium with a parietal layer lining the fibrous sac and a visceral layer (epicardium) adherent directly to the heart surface, with the slim pericardial cavity and its lubricating pericardial fluid between them. The heart wall itself has three layers: the epicardium (the same visceral pericardial layer), the thick myocardium (cardiac muscle, doing the actual pumping work — and internally reinforced by a dense connective-tissue fibrous skeleton that anchors the valves, electrically insulates the atria from the ventricles, and serves as the origin/insertion for cardiac muscle fibers), and the smooth inner endocardium lining the chambers and valves.

The heart has four chambers: two thin-walled atria that receive incoming blood (the right atrium from the venae cavae and coronary sinus; the left atrium from the pulmonary veins) and two thicker ventricles that pump blood out (the right ventricle to the pulmonary trunk; the left ventricle, by far the most muscular chamber, out to the aorta and the entire systemic circulation). Blood flows one-way through two valve types. The atrioventricular (AV) valves — the tricuspid valve (right) and bicuspid/mitral valve (left) — prevent backflow from ventricle to atrium during contraction, held closed by chordae tendineae tethered to papillary muscles projecting from the ventricular wall. The semilunar valves — pulmonary valve and aortic valve — prevent backflow from the great vessels into the ventricles during relaxation, and (unlike the AV valves) require no chordae tendineae because their cup-shaped cusps simply fill with backflowing blood to snap shut. Both the SA and AV nodes, along with the myocardium generally, receive cardiac innervation from the autonomic cardiac plexus (sympathetic and vagal parasympathetic fibers), which modulates rate and force but does not initiate the heartbeat.

3.2 Conduction, Cardiac Cycle, Coronary Vessels & the Aorta IO 2–3

The heartbeat is initiated and coordinated by the cardiac conduction system: the sinoatrial (SA) node, in the right atrial wall, is the heart's intrinsic pacemaker, generating the electrical impulse that spreads across both atria; the signal then converges on the atrioventricular (AV) node, which briefly delays it (allowing the atria to finish contracting before the ventricles begin), before it travels down the AV bundle (bundle of His), splits into the right and left bundle branches, and spreads through the ventricular myocardium via Purkinje fibers. This electrical sequence drives the cardiac cycle's two phases — systole (contraction, ejecting blood) and diastole (relaxation, filling with blood) — with the AV valves closing (heart sound S1) at the start of ventricular systole and the semilunar valves closing (S2) at its end. Autonomic regulation of heart rate layers on top of this intrinsic rhythm: sympathetic input increases SA node firing rate and contractility, while parasympathetic (vagal) input slows it.

The myocardium is itself supplied by the coronary blood vessels, the heart's own circulation, arising as the first branches off the ascending aorta just above the aortic valve. The left coronary artery divides into the anterior interventricular (LAD) branch (supplying the anterior interventricular septum and much of the left ventricle) and the circumflex branch (wrapping around the left side of the heart); the right coronary artery gives off a posterior interventricular branch and marginal branches, supplying the right ventricle and (typically) the SA/AV nodes. Cardiac veins largely parallel the coronary arteries and drain into the coronary sinus, a large venous channel on the heart's posterior surface that empties directly into the right atrium.

Leaving the heart, the aorta is the body's largest artery, traced in four parts. The ascending aorta gives rise only to the coronary arteries before curving as the aortic arch, whose three branches — the brachiocephalic trunk (splitting into the right subclavian and right common carotid arteries), the left common carotid artery, and the left subclavian artery — supply the head, neck, and upper limbs. The thoracic (descending) aorta runs through the posterior mediastinum giving off paired branches to the thoracic wall and viscera (posterior intercostal, bronchial, and esophageal arteries) before passing through the diaphragm's aortic hiatus to become the abdominal aorta. The abdominal aorta gives off three unpaired branches to the gut — the celiac trunk (immediately below the diaphragm, splitting into the left gastric, splenic, and common hepatic arteries to supply the foregut), the superior mesenteric artery (supplying the midgut), and the inferior mesenteric artery (supplying the hindgut) — alongside paired branches including the renal and gonadal (testicular/ovarian) arteries, before terminating as the paired common iliac arteries, each dividing into an internal iliac artery (pelvic viscera) and an external iliac artery (continuing as the femoral artery to supply the lower limb).

4 · Arteries, Veins & Lymphatic Systems

Instructional Objectives

  1. Compare the histology and types of arteries, veins, and capillaries, and describe mechanisms that assist venous return.
  2. Describe the composition of blood.
  3. Describe the organization and function of the lymphatic system.

4.1 Vessel Histology, Types & Venous Return IO 1

Blood vessel walls share a common three-layer plan: an inner tunica intima (a single layer of endothelium plus a thin connective-tissue layer), a middle tunica media (smooth muscle and elastic fibers, by far the thickest layer in arteries, responsible for vasoconstriction/vasodilation), and an outer tunica externa (adventitia) (connective tissue anchoring the vessel to surrounding structures). Arteries are subdivided by size and wall composition into elastic (conducting) arteries (large, near the heart — aorta and its major branches — with abundant elastic fibers that recoil between heartbeats to keep blood flowing smoothly), muscular (distributing) arteries (most named arteries, with a thick smooth-muscle media that actively regulates blood flow to specific regions), and arterioles (the smallest arteries, acting as the primary resistance vessels that control blood pressure and flow into capillary beds). Capillaries, built from a single endothelial layer, are the only vessels thin enough to allow the exchange of gases, nutrients, and wastes with tissue.

Compared to their arterial counterparts, veins have thinner walls (especially a much thinner tunica media) and larger lumens, and many — particularly in the limbs — contain venous valves, one-way flaps that prevent the backflow of blood against gravity. Because venous pressure alone is often insufficient to return blood to the heart, especially from the lower body, venous return is assisted by the skeletal muscle pump (contracting limb muscles compress adjacent veins, and the valves ensure the resulting flow is directed toward the heart) and the respiratory pump (pressure changes in the thorax and abdomen during breathing promote venous flow toward the heart). Venous drainage of the head/neck/upper limbs converges as the paired brachiocephalic veins, which join to form the superior vena cava; drainage from the lower body converges as the inferior vena cava. The azygos system (the azygos vein on the right, joined by the hemiazygos and accessory hemiazygos veins on the left, which cross the midline to join it) provides an important collateral pathway draining the posterior thoracic and abdominal walls into the superior vena cava, bypassing the inferior vena cava if it becomes obstructed.

4.2 Blood Composition & the Lymphatic System IO 2–3

Whole blood is composed of plasma (roughly 55% of blood volume — water, electrolytes, and plasma proteins like albumin, globulins, and clotting factors) and the formed elements (roughly 45%): red blood cells (erythrocytes) (biconcave, anucleate cells carrying oxygen via hemoglobin — by far the most numerous formed element), white blood cells (leukocytes) (the immune system's mobile defenders — neutrophils, lymphocytes, monocytes, eosinophils, and basophils), and platelets (cell fragments essential for clot formation).

The lymphatic system serves three major functions: returning excess interstitial fluid to the bloodstream (maintaining fluid balance), providing sites for immune surveillance and response, and absorbing dietary lipids from the small intestine. Lymph (the fluid within lymphatic vessels) begins in blind-ended lymphatic capillaries, which — unlike blood capillaries — are more permeable, allowing large proteins and even cells to enter. From there, lymph passes through valved lymphatic vessels (structurally similar to veins, but generally thinner-walled) organized into superficial and deep sets, which filter through lymph nodes along the way (where immune cells screen and respond to lymph contents) before draining into larger lymphatic trunks. Nearly all lymph below the diaphragm and from the left side of the body above it converges into the thoracic duct, which empties into the venous system near the junction of the left internal jugular and left subclavian veins; lymph from the right upper body drains via the smaller right lymphatic duct into the corresponding right-sided venous junction. Because the lymphatic system has no central pump, lymph propulsion relies on the same skeletal muscle and respiratory pump mechanisms as venous return, along with intrinsic rhythmic contraction of smooth muscle in the walls of larger lymphatic vessels, all directed one-way by internal valves. Key lymphoid organs and tissues include the spleen (filters blood and mounts immune responses to blood-borne pathogens), the thymus (site of T-lymphocyte maturation), and the tonsils (guard the pharyngeal entrance to the airway/digestive tract).

5 · Pulmonary Anatomy / Respiratory System

Instructional Objectives

  1. Describe the structures of the upper respiratory tract, including the nasal cavity, pharynx, and larynx.
  2. Describe the lower respiratory tract's branching organization from the trachea to the alveoli, and distinguish the conducting from the respiratory zone.
  3. Describe the gross anatomy of the lungs and pleura, the muscles of respiration, and the pulmonary/bronchial blood supply.

5.1 Upper Respiratory Tract, Larynx & Trachea IO 1

The respiratory system's overall functions — gas exchange, pH regulation, protection against inhaled pathogens/particles, sound production, and olfaction — are carried out by structures divided into the upper respiratory tract (nose through larynx) and lower respiratory tract (trachea through alveoli). Incoming air is warmed, humidified, and filtered as it passes through the nasal cavity, divided in the midline by the nasal septum and lined by respiratory epithelium (ciliated pseudostratified columnar epithelium with mucus-secreting goblet cells) that traps particles and moves them (via ciliary beating) toward the pharynx for swallowing — a major mechanism of respiratory tract protection.

Air then passes through the pharynx, subdivided into three regions: the nasopharynx (posterior to the nasal cavity, housing the pharyngeal tonsil/adenoid and the opening of the auditory tube), the oropharynx (posterior to the oral cavity, housing the palatine and lingual tonsils, shared by air and food), and the laryngopharynx (continuing down to the larynx and esophagus, also shared by both pathways) — in contrast, the larynx itself is a dedicated airway, distinct from the pharynx in that it's built of cartilage and carries only air, not food. The larynx's cartilaginous framework includes three unpaired cartilages — the large thyroid cartilage (forming the laryngeal prominence/Adam's apple), the ring-shaped cricoid cartilage (the only complete cartilaginous ring in the airway, inferior to the thyroid cartilage), and the leaf-shaped elastic epiglottis (folds down over the laryngeal inlet during swallowing to direct food into the esophagus, with the vallecula — a small mucosal pocket between the tongue base and epiglottis — collecting saliva/secretions just anterior to it) — and three paired cartilages, most notably the pyramid-shaped arytenoid cartilages (posteriorly, anchoring the vocal folds and rotating to open/close them), plus the smaller corniculate and cuneiform cartilages.

The laryngeal musculature (intrinsic muscles acting on the arytenoid cartilages) adjusts vocal fold tension and the width of the airway opening, enabling sound production: air forced between the tensed vocal folds (true vocal cords) sets them vibrating to produce sound, while the vestibular folds (false vocal cords), sitting just above them, play a protective role rather than a phonatory one; together with the space between the vocal folds (the rima glottidis), the vocal folds form the glottis.

Below the larynx, the trachea is supported by roughly 15–20 C-shaped rings of hyaline cartilage — open posteriorly, where a band of smooth trachealis muscle instead spans the gap, allowing the adjacent esophagus to bulge slightly forward during swallowing. Like the nasal cavity, the trachea is lined by ciliated pseudostratified respiratory epithelium that continues the mucociliary escalator down toward the bronchi (a helpful contrast: this pseudostratified columnar epithelium looks layered because nuclei sit at different heights, but every cell actually contacts the basement membrane — unlike true stratified columnar epithelium, which is rare in the body).

5.2 Lungs, Pleura & Respiratory Muscles IO 2–3

The trachea terminates by dividing into the right and left primary (main) bronchi — the right primary bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertically oriented than the left, making it the more common site for aspirated foreign objects to lodge. Each primary bronchus branches into secondary (lobar) bronchi (three on the right, one per lobe; two on the left), which branch further into tertiary (segmental) bronchi — each supplying an independent, surgically resectable unit of lung tissue called a bronchopulmonary segment. This marks the boundary between the airway's conducting zone (trachea through terminal bronchioles — moves air but performs no gas exchange) and its respiratory zone (respiratory bronchioles onward, where gas exchange begins): segmental bronchi continue branching into progressively smaller bronchioles, then terminal bronchioles, then respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, and finally clusters of alveoli — the thin-walled sacs where alveolar gas exchange actually occurs, lined mostly by thin type I pneumocytes (for efficient diffusion) with scattered type II pneumocytes that secrete surfactant to reduce surface tension and keep alveoli from collapsing.

The right lung has three lobes (superior, middle, inferior) separated by an oblique and a horizontal fissure, while the left lung has only two lobes (superior, inferior) separated by a single oblique fissure — reflecting the space the heart occupies on the left, which also produces the left lung's distinctive cardiac notch and small tongue-like lingula along its anterior-inferior border. On the medial surface of each lung, the hilum is the point where the bronchus, pulmonary vessels, bronchial vessels, and nerves all enter/exit — collectively called the root of the lung.

Each lung is invested in a visceral pleura and separately housed within a parietal pleura lining the thoracic wall, together forming the pleural cavity — a thin potential space containing lubricating pleural fluid that both reduces friction during breathing and, through its surface tension, couples the lung surface tightly to the chest wall so the lung expands as the thorax does. Quiet inhalation is driven primarily by the diaphragm (contracting and flattening to increase thoracic volume) and the external intercostal muscles (elevating the ribs); forced breathing recruits accessory respiratory muscles (scalenes and sternocleidomastoid for forced inhalation; internal intercostals and abdominal wall muscles for forced exhalation). The diaphragm itself is pierced by three key openings (hiatuses) at different vertebral levels: the caval opening (T8, for the inferior vena cava), the esophageal hiatus (T10, for the esophagus and vagus nerves), and the aortic hiatus (T12, for the aorta, thoracic duct, and azygos vein) — a helpful mnemonic is that the vertebral level roughly matches the number of letters in each structure's name (vena cava=8, esophagus=10, aortic=12). Lung tissue itself receives two separate blood supplies: the pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood to the alveoli for gas exchange, while separate bronchial arteries (branches of the thoracic aorta) supply oxygenated blood to the bronchial tree and connective tissue framework of the lung itself, with bronchial veins partially draining into the azygos system. Respiratory reflexes (such as the cough reflex and the Hering-Breuer inflation reflex) provide additional automatic protection and regulation of the breathing cycle.
